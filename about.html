The Project

The Wild Stream is a didactic project, whose main aim is to showcase, with four short experimental footages, the huge dangers of the Elsagate phenomenon occuring online.
Directly downloaded from Youtube, following the Subreddit reports, we edited the uncontrolled content in order to convey, in a more clear way, this mysterious, yet well defined, iter Elsagate videos follow.
To present our series we needed a language that rhymed with the problem, without being as unrestrained as the content itself, which is already extremely frightening.
We decided to tone it a little bit down by adding light references to childhood, summed up by stickers buttons and tickets frames, along with an interactive navigation that allows the user to discover the different layers our project consists of.

The Research
With the project "the Tube Trap"we focused on Youtube’s content farm, especially on those contents addressed to kids, where we found recursive patterns across the published videos (e.g. same rhymes, images and videos formats), along with the dangerous Elsagate phenomenon.

We pushed our research a little bit further, a little bit further, aiming at making parents’ aware of this really subtle and mostly unknown phenomenon their kids may encounter whilst spending time on YouTube.
To do so we created four short experimental and intentionally shocking videos to visually explain how this phenomenon appears and works on YouTube.

All the material arranged for the videos comes from Youtube.

But how did we find such inappropriate material?

Also for this phase, we started from the Reddit platform, a network of communities based on people's interests, where registered users discuss various topics and upload content (links, post, images, videos).
Reddit is a huge world so we decided to focus on a smaller section, the r/Subreddit, an organization of content divided into boards that gathers, in one place, all the related material about different topics, normally created by users.
Elsagate phenomenon was originally reported on its dedicated Subreddit, from parents who noticed inappropriate kid content on Youtube.

We decided to get our material right from there, since, still today, it represents a powerful way to report misleading contents.
Clicking on the r/elsagate we got access to all the reports from the users who joined that subreddit. Here, people publish mainly screens of problematic channels or dangerous videos.

The dataset creation

We created a dataset with all the information we found useful such as videos authors, channels’ name, characters involved, reported issue in the footage and we created a short clip to summarize the problematic video.
These clips are gathered in the archive, whereas you can find the videos in the main page.

The number of posts related to the Elsagate phenomenon is pretty big, so we decided to center our analysis on four symbolic characters: Frozen Elsa, because her character gave name to the phenomenon we’re studying, Spiderman and superheroes, since there’s still a great interest for them, across children from different ages, Peppa Pig, because it’s a very popular cartoon among little kids and lastly, we chose Minecraft, since we thought it would be interesting to study content addressed to elder kids.
By choosing these four we also manage to cover almost all the audience’s ages: 2-4 years old, 5-8 and 9+, providing a sort of scaled YouTube model.

Elsagate

First steps to understand the phenomenon

For a better understanding of the Elsagate phenomenon,
it is important to introduce the former step: the content farm one.
Mysterious companies upload a huge amount of low-quality videos, characterized by massive production and content repetition, designed to satisfy the logic of YouTube’s algorithm and recommendations criteria. In this way creators get more views and more money, with the least effort. Just imagine video content farmers making videos as they were producing clothes in huge factories: producing millions of same patterns t-shirts is by far more convenient than designing millions of different motifs. This is why content farmers take advantage from replicating the same characters, sound and effects across different videos.


Why is this happening?

The reasons behind these videos are related to the revenue people or companies running these channels get from views; for each view obtained, more money is gained. Views are also important to determine the price of advertising slots, which often interrupt YouTube videos and appear to the watchers, just like tv spots. Studies demonstrate that 81% of children under the age of 11, use, more or less regularly, YouTube. Being this young audience so active, a huge quantity of content addressed to it is produced,
for a better chance to reach popularity.

Yes but why “Elsa”?

Videos uploaded on YouTube are so many that the system does not manage to supervise them all. Content farm videos offer a great contribution to this huge flow and, among them, some are targeted to children and are also inappropriate. Under “inappropriate'' go all those videos that present shocking, repugnant, violent free of charge and obscene contents. In this scenario, in 2014, a dangerous phenomenon, later called “Elsagate”, started to spread. The term refers to all those inappropriate videos that exploit well known kids' characters (e.g. cartoons characters and heroes) to convey misleading content.
In 2014, a video titled “Disney Princess Dolls Goes on a Date!"was published and it featured Frozen’s Elsa as the protagonist, together with Ken and Spiderman dolls acting strangely, with veiled sexual references: it reached 37.384.055 views. From this moment on, this method has been extended to many other characters, but Elsa was the first one used with the purpose of attracting young viewers, giving the name to the phenomenon.

Who’s behind the phenomenon?

At the moment it's almost impossible to figure out where these videos are coming from and who makes them: it could either beteams of professional animatorsas well as softwares that generates them randomly. Without knowing the sources, it is hard to prevent them from publishing these contents. What is clear though is the fact that those who produce them have for sure economic reasons: videos that reach high numbers of views
call the attention of advertisers, who see these media as an excellent monetizing source.
